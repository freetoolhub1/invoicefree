How to Send an Invoice as a Freelancer (Step-by-Step Guide)

By InvoiceFree.info ·April 28, 2026 ·🕒 8 min read

Sending your first invoice as a freelancer feels more complicated than it should. What exactly needs to go on it? How do you send it — email, PDF, link? What payment terms should you use? And what do you do when a client ignores it?

This guide answers all of it. By the end, you'll have a clear process for creating, sending, and following up on invoices — starting with your next project.

Step-by-Step: How to Create and Send a Freelance Invoice

1
Agree on scope and price before you start work

The invoice is the end of the payment process, not the beginning. Before you do a single hour of work, confirm the deliverables, price, and payment terms in writing — even a short email thread works. This prevents disputes when the invoice arrives.

2
Create the invoice the same day you deliver

Every day you wait to invoice is a day added to your wait for payment. The moment you send the final deliverable — whether that's a design file, a piece of writing, a completed website, or a consulting report — open your invoice tool and create the invoice immediately. Clients are most responsive and most satisfied right after receiving finished work.

3
Fill in all required fields

A complete freelance invoice must include: your name and contact details, client name and company, a unique invoice number, the issue date, a clear due date, itemised line items with description and price, any applicable tax, the total due, and your payment details. Missing any of these creates friction — and friction delays payment.

4
Set the right payment terms

For most freelance work, Net 7 (due within 7 days) is the right default. Start there. Many clients will accept it without question — and those who push back give you room to negotiate to Net 14. Only accept Net 30 for large, established companies where the relationship justifies the wait. Never default to Net 30 for new clients.

5
Export as a PDF

Send invoices as PDF files — never as editable Word documents or Google Docs links. PDFs cannot be accidentally modified, they print cleanly, and they look professional. Use an invoice tool that exports a clean PDF without watermarks. InvoiceFree.info does this for free.

6
Send via email with a clear subject line

Attach the PDF to an email. Use a clear, professional subject line — something like Invoice #INV-0042 — [Your Name] — Due [Date]. The invoice number and due date in the subject line make it easy to find, easy to file, and harder to ignore.

7
Follow up if you don't hear back

Send a short follow-up email three days after the due date if the invoice hasn't been paid. Keep it brief and polite. Most late payments are resolved with a single follow-up — clients often simply forget.

What to Write in the Invoice Email

The email itself should be short. The invoice contains all the details — the email just needs to flag what's being sent and make it easy to act on:

Subject: Invoice #INV-0042 — Web Design Project — Due 12 May 2026 Hi Sarah, Please find attached Invoice #INV-0042 for the homepage redesign project, due on 12 May 2026. Total due: $1,800.00 Payment can be made via bank transfer to the details on the invoice. Let me know if you have any questions. Thank you, [Your Name]

That's it. No lengthy summary of the work, no apology for sending an invoice. Clean, direct, professional.

Always include your payment details on the invoice itself Clients forward invoices to their accountants and bookkeepers, who never saw your original email. If your bank details or PayPal address are only in the email body — not on the PDF — payment gets delayed while someone tracks down your contact info.

How to Handle Late Payments

Late payments are frustrating, but they're common — around 29% of freelance invoices are paid after the due date. Having a clear escalation process removes the awkwardness and gets you paid faster.

Day 3 after due date — first nudge:

Subject: Invoice #INV-0042 — Following Up Hi Sarah, Just a quick note — Invoice #INV-0042 for $1,800 was due on 12 May. Could you let me know when I can expect payment? Thanks, [Your Name]

Day 10 after due date — firmer follow-up: Reference the specific invoice number, amount, and original due date. Mention that the late payment clause on the invoice applies (a 1.5% monthly fee, if you included one). Offer to help resolve any issues if there's a dispute.

Day 30+ — formal notice: At this stage, a more formal email noting the outstanding amount and a final payment deadline is appropriate. For amounts over $1,000, this is also the point at which you may want to consult a local small claims or debt recovery process.

Common Mistakes When Sending Freelance Invoices

  • Sending the invoice too late. Invoicing a week or two after delivery signals that you're not tracking payments carefully — and clients pick up on that.
  • Vague line items. "Design work — $2,000" is not an itemised invoice. Break it down: "Homepage redesign (3 rounds of revisions) — $1,400" and "Mobile responsive adaptation — $600."
  • Missing payment details. If a client has to email you to find out how to pay, the payment is already delayed.
  • Using Net 30 by default. Most freelancers accept Net 30 without negotiating because it feels standard. It isn't a requirement — it's just a habit. Net 7 is perfectly normal for most freelance work.
  • Not keeping copies. Save every invoice you send with the date and amount. You'll need this for tax season and for any payment disputes.
